Welcome to AutoLab Annotator

Thank you so much for your time to help us in this project! Please read the instructions below carefully before you begin.

1 Your Task

Assume you are explaining a video of a scientific experiment to a grad student with visual impairment who cannot see the scene, but needs to know the critical steps. You should describe everything in a way that allows the student to reconstruct the experiment.

Your explanation should be complete, structured, and sequential, as if you are narrating what is happening in real time.

Why this matters: We will use your annotation to grade a Vision-Language Model (VLM) that is challenged with the same task, so the more informative and precise your description, the better.

2 What to Include in Each Step

Walk the student through the procedure the same way you'd walk them through it in person. For every step, try to convey:

  • What is being used: the instruments, containers, reagents, and samples involved. The first time something appears, briefly say what it is so the student can picture it (e.g., "a P200 micropipette," "a 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube with a clear colorless liquid inside").
  • What is being done: the action itself. Please use specific action verbs like pipettes, aspirates, dispenses, vortexes, inverts, decants, centrifuges, weighs, dissolves, transfers, incubates, rather than vague ones like uses, puts, or does. The student is relying on the verb to understand the motion, so "she vortexes the tube for 5 seconds" tells them much more than "she mixes it."
  • Measurable values: any volumes, masses, concentrations, temperatures, durations, or speeds that are visible on screen, shown on a display, written on a label, or said out loud.
  • Visible changes: color shifts, precipitation, bubbling, phase separation, dissolution, anything observable that signals something is happening.
  • How things come together: how the tools and materials interact, e.g., "the tube is loaded into the centrifuge rotor and the lid is closed."
Don't skip routine steps like attaching a pipette tip or capping a tube. The student can't see them, so to them they aren't routine, they're part of the procedure.

3 When You're Not Sure

Only describe what you can actually see in the video. Please don't fill things in from your own knowledge of how the protocol "should" go, even if you're confident. We want the annotation to reflect the video, not the textbook.

A few cases come up often:

  • A parameter would normally be specified, but the video doesn't show or state it. Mark it as [Not Visible Parameter].
    Example: "She centrifuges the tube for [Not Visible Parameter] at [Not Visible Parameter] rpm."
  • Something is happening but you can't quite tell what. Mark it [Unclear] and describe what you can see.
    Example: "She adds [Unclear: appears to be a white powder] to the flask."
  • You can't confidently identify a reagent or instrument. Describe its observable properties instead of guessing the name.
    Example: Write "a clear colorless liquid in a glass vial" rather than naming a specific reagent you're unsure about.

4 Output Format

Please list steps in order within each segment. Use the following format:

Example:
Step 1: She attaches a disposable tip to a P200 micropipette.
Step 2: She aspirates 20 µL from a 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube containing a clear colorless liquid and dispenses it into a second microcentrifuge tube.
Step 3: She caps the receiving tube and inverts it three times to mix.

5 How This Tool Works

  1. Each video is split into 30-second segments.
  2. Watch a segment using the Play button.
  3. Write your step-by-step description in the text box on the right.
  4. Click Save (or simply move to the next segment, it auto-saves).
  5. Repeat for every segment. Use the Video dropdown to switch between videos.
  6. When finished, click Export JSON to download your annotations and send the file to the project lead.
Keyboard shortcuts: Space = play/pause, Left/Right arrows = previous/next segment. Your progress is auto-saved in your browser. You can close the page and come back anytime.
